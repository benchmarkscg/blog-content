## *“Ok… but what does a real risk assessment actually look like?”*

Last time we talked about ISO 12100 and how it structures a risk assessment.  
But theory is easy. Standing in front of a real machine — that’s where things get interesting.

If you’ve ever looked at a machine and wondered *where do I even start?*, this walkthrough is for you.

---

## <i class="bi bi-gear-wide-connected"></i> The machine

Here’s the machine exactly as it exists today — no guards, no interlocks, just raw mechanics.

![Raw machine without safeguards](/assets/img/iso_12100_raw.webp)

It’s a rotary indexing table with an orbital riveting head.  
It’s been running for years. Parts are produced. Nobody questions it.  
At first glance, it *works*.

But ISO 12100 doesn’t ask if it works. It asks: **is it safe?**

---

## <i class="bi bi-bounding-box"></i> Defining the limits

Before identifying hazards, you need to define the limits of the machine — ISO 12100’s first step.

### Space limits  
Where does the machine physically start and end?

![Machine limits volume](/assets/img/iso_12100_step_1.webp)

### Use & access limits  
Where does the operator interact?  
Where does maintenance occur?  
What areas are intentionally reachable?

![Machine limits access zones](/assets/img/iso_12100_step_1_1.webp)

Without clear limits, there is no risk assessment — just guesswork.

---

## <i class="bi bi-exclamation-triangle-fill"></i> Identifying hazards

Once you start looking closely, hazards appear everywhere.

![Hazard zones](/assets/img/iso_12100_step_2.webp)

Some examples:

- The point of operation → **crushing risk**  
- The rotating table → **entrapment**  
- Interfaces between moving and fixed parts → **shearing**  

And the most dangerous part?  
The operator’s natural working position is right next to these hazards.

A typical scenario:  
> During part loading, the operator’s hand enters the hazard zone while the table is still indexing.

This is exactly the kind of real‑world situation ISO 12100 forces you to see.

---

## <i class="bi bi-shield-check"></i> Reducing the risks

Now we apply the hierarchy of risk reduction — in the order ISO 12100 requires:

1. **Inherently safe design** (minimize exposure where possible)  
2. **Safeguarding & protective measures**  
3. **Information for use**  

![Safeguarded machine](/assets/img/iso_12100_step_3.webp)

For this machine, that means:

- Physical guards to prevent access  
- Interlocked doors  
- Two-hand control  
- Emergency stop  
- Light curtains where needed  

Same machine. Completely different risk level.

---

## <i class="bi bi-zoom-in"></i> Where risk actually lives

Risk is not spread evenly across a machine.  
It concentrates in very specific points — like the point of operation.

![Point of operation before and after](/assets/img/iso_12100_zoom_operation.webp)

If you don’t control the point of operation, you haven’t controlled the machine.

---

## <i class="bi bi-lightbulb"></i> Final thought

This is what ISO 12100 really does.  
It doesn’t give you answers. It gives you a way to see.

And once you see the hazards clearly, the decisions become much harder to ignore.
