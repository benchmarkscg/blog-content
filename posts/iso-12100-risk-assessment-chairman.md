## *“I need to evaluate a machine… where the hell do I even start?”*

If you’ve ever asked yourself that question, you’re not alone.  
We’ve all been there: standing in front of a machine that has been running for years, with operators who no longer fear it, with undocumented modifications, and with the quiet pressure of knowing that whatever you decide has real consequences.

![Production Line Machinery](/assets/img/ISO_12100_example_machine.webp)

And right there — in that moment of uncertainty — ISO 12100 steps in as the **chairman** of the entire process.  
It’s not the longest standard, nor the most technical, nor the most intimidating.  
But it’s the one that **brings order to the chaos**.

![Risk Assessment checklist](/assets/img/ISO_12100_checklist.jpg)

So, in the big picture, ISO 12100 basically says:

1. **Identify *all* hazards.**  
   Every single one.  
   If you can scratch your finger on it, write it down.  
   Hazards are potential sources of harm — crushing, pinching, cutting, shearing, breaking, sliding, burning… and a long list of etc.  
   Any potential source of damage, at any level, should be identified.  
   Later, those hazards will be evaluated as **risks** (probability × severity).

![Hazards listed under ISO 12100](/assets/img/ISO_12100_Hazards.webp)

2. **Estimate and rate the risks.**  
   ISO 12100 doesn’t prescribe a single method; it only requires that you consider both the probability of occurrence and the severity of harm.  
   Use any method you like — Hazard Rating Number (HRN) is a personal favorite because it’s simple, fast, and brutally honest.  
   Other methods lean on color‑coded scales, which can get ambiguous.  
   It’s much easier to say *“this is a 100‑HRN risk”* than *“this is… orange‑ish.”*  
   Whichever works better for you, both are valid under ISO 12100.

![risk estimation matrix](/assets/img/risk-assessment-matrix-risk-estimation.png)

![hazard rating number formula](/assets/img/ISO_12100_HRN.png)

3. **Evaluate your risks.**  
   Is anything one point too high?  
   Is the residual risk acceptable?  
   If the answer is “no,” then…

4. **Reduce the risks.**  
   ISO 12100 is crystal clear about the hierarchy:  
   - **Best:** inherent safety by design. This might sound obvious but
     If you don’t need a 10,000‑rpm motor, don’t use one.  
     If your machine works perfectly at 80 PSI, don’t push a 300‑PSI cylinder just because it’s available.  
   - **If you can’t eliminate it:** apply technical protective measures — guards, light curtains, two‑hand controls.  
   - **If risk still remains (and it will):** provide information — warnings, trainings, and PPE.  
     Risk zero is a fantasy; risk *reduction* is the goal.

![iso 12100 hierarchy of controls](/assets/img/ISO_12100_Hierarchy_Of_Controls.png)

5. **Re‑evaluate.**  
   After mitigation, is the risk low enough?  
   If not, loop back. Identify all hazards again — did your mitigation create new ones? Check, adjust, and keep going.  
   If yes — congratulations, you’ve actually completed a real risk assessment.

And here’s the real deal:

**Document. Every. Step.**  
How was the machine when you first saw it?  
What hazards did you find?  
How did you rate them?  
What mitigation did you propose?  
What was the risk *after* mitigation?

Everything matters — because the assessment isn’t just a form.  
It’s the story of how a machine becomes safer.  

---

A risk assessment isn’t a one‑and‑done checklist.  
It’s iterative by design.  
You’re supposed to circle back, challenge your own assumptions, and adjust when reality doesn’t match the diagram.

Maybe a mitigation didn’t reduce the risk as much as you expected.  
Maybe a guard introduced a new pinch point.  
Maybe the operator found a “creative” way to bypass your solution in less than 24 hours.  
(It happens more often than anyone wants to admit.)

That’s why ISO 12100 keeps pushing you through the cycle again and again — not to annoy you, but because machines evolve, processes evolve, and people definitely evolve.

The goal isn’t perfection.  
The goal is **continuous, documented improvement**.

And once you start seeing the process that way, the whole thing stops feeling like bureaucracy and starts feeling like what it really is:

A structured way to make sure the machine — and the people around it — stay safe.
