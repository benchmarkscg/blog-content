# The 7 Most Common Mistakes in Machinery Risk Assessments — And How to Avoid Them

A few days ago, I walked into a plant where a brand‑new automated cell had been shut down for three days.

Not because of a mechanical failure — but because the risk assessment had overlooked a single, predictable operator behavior.

One small oversight.  
Thousands of dollars in downtime.  
And a frustrated production team.  

Risk assessments are powerful tools — but only when they reflect reality.

And here are the same seven mistakes that show up again and again when assessing machinery.

Once you see them, you can’t unsee them.

---

## Why Machinery Risk Assessments Fail in Practice

Most teams don’t fail because they ignore safety.  
They fail because their assessments are disconnected from how machines are *actually* used.

Below are the **seven most common mistakes** I see in the field — and how to avoid them.

## 1. Not Involving Real Operators {#not-involving-operators}

![Engineer and operator discussing workflow](/assets/img/common-mistakes-01.webp)

I’ve been guilty of this myself — designing from the desk and assuming the workflow would play out exactly as I imagined. But machines don’t live in renders.
They live on the shop floor, surrounded by pressure, habits, and humans.

And operators? They know the machine better than anyone.

I’ve lost count of how many times an operator has shown me a “workaround” that completely changed the risk profile of a task.

**Why it matters**  
If you don’t include operators, you’re assessing a machine that only exists in your head.

**What to do instead**
- Go to the floor — do a real gemba  
- Observe at least one full production cycle  
- Ask operators: *“What usually goes wrong?”*

> The closer you get to real workflows, the clearer the risks become.

And when you ask operators what usually goes wrong, the answers almost 
always point to non‑routine tasks — the jams, the fixture changes, 
the adjustments. And that leads straight into the next mistake.

---

## 2. Ignoring Non-Routine Tasks {#ignoring-non-routine-tasks}

![Maintenance worker performing a non-routine task on a machine](/assets/img/common-mistakes-02.webp)

Normal operation is rarely where injuries happen.  
The danger shows up during the messy moments — the jams, the adjustments, the tool changes, the troubleshooting.

**Why it matters**  
These tasks often require bypassing safeguards or entering hazardous zones directly.

**What to do instead**
- Map the **full lifecycle** of the machine  
- Include maintenance, setup, and recovery tasks  
- Evaluate access to hazardous zones during these scenarios  

> If you don’t assess non‑routine tasks, you’re missing the highest‑risk moments in the machine’s life.

---

## 3. Confusing Severity With Probability {#severity-vs-probability}

![Risk matrix highlighting high-severity low-probability quadrant](/assets/img/common-mistakes-03.webp)

I often see teams downplay hazards because “it almost never happens.”  
But that’s not how risk works.

A hazard is the source of potential harm.  
Risk is the combination of **how bad** and **how likely**.

A low‑severity, high‑probability event can be just as risky as a high‑severity, low‑probability one.  
And catastrophic hazards remain critical — even if unlikely.

**Why it matters**  
Misunderstanding this leads to underestimating the most dangerous hazards.

**What to do instead**
- Treat severity as a primary driver  
- Avoid “it rarely happens” as justification  
- Use structured methods to evaluate risk consistently  

> When you separate severity from probability, the real priorities reveal themselves.

Here’s where tools like the Hazard Rating Number get useful.

## 4. Overlooking Foreseeable Misuse {#foreseeable-misuse}

![Safety gate slightly open with mannequin operator reaching inside](/assets/img/common-mistakes-04.webp)

I’ve watched operators bypass systems with zip ties, magnets, even folded paper — not because they wanted to, but because the design boxed them into it.

People don’t misuse machines out of malice.  
They do it because the workflow, the pressure, or the design makes the “wrong” way the easiest way.

**Why it matters**  
If a misuse is foreseeable, it must be part of the assessment.

**What to do instead**
- Identify **foreseeable misuse scenarios**  
- Design systems that tolerate real behavior  
- Reduce incentives to bypass safeguards  

> If it can be done, it will be done.


And this leads to another trap I see constantly: assuming a control that worked once will work everywhere.

---

## 5. Copying Controls Without Validation {#copying-controls}

![Two similar machines with different guard configurations](/assets/img/common-mistakes-05.webp)

Two machines may look identical — until you watch how they’re actually used.

I’ve seen teams copy a guard or safety function from one machine to another, assuming it will behave the same. It rarely does.

Different tasks.  
Different operators.  
Different cycle times.  
Different access points.  
Different failure modes.

**Why it matters**  
Controls must be validated in context — not copied blindly.
Standards such as ISO 13849 and IEC 62061 explicitly requires validation
within the actual application context.

**What to do instead**
- Reassess risks for each machine  
- Validate safety functions under real conditions  
- Avoid “copy‑paste engineering”  

> Context isn’t optional — it’s the difference between a control that works and one that fails silently.

But even the best controls fall apart if no one remembers why they were chosen in the first place.
So document everything.

---

## 6. Poor Documentation {#poor-documentation}

![Desk with risk assessment documents and notes](/assets/img/common-mistakes-06.webp)

Documentation isn’t paperwork — it’s the story of the machine.

Without it, teams end up guessing why a guard was placed where it was, why a sensor was selected, or why a risk was accepted. I’ve seen audits derail simply because no one could explain a decision made years earlier.

**Why it matters**
- audits become difficult  
- updates become inconsistent  
- decisions cannot be justified  

Poor or missing documentation is consistently one of the top audit findings.

**What to do instead**
- Document *why* each decision was made  
- Link hazards to standards and controls  
- Ensure traceability across revisions  
- Keep documentation accessible  

> Good documentation isn’t bureaucracy — it’s engineering memory.


And finally, even beautifully documented assessments become useless the moment the machine changes — which it always does.

---

## 7. Not Updating the Assessment After Changes {#not-updating}

![Machine with highlighted new component affecting safety](/assets/img/common-mistakes-07.webp)

Machines evolve.  
New tooling, new software, new operators, new cycle times — each change shifts the risk profile.

I’ve seen machines run for years with outdated assessments that no longer reflect reality.  
And when something goes wrong, the gap becomes painfully obvious.

**Why it matters**  
A risk assessment is not a one‑time deliverable.

**What to do instead**
- Treat the assessment as a **living document**  
- Trigger reviews after any modification  
- Align documentation with actual machine state  

> A risk assessment that isn’t updated becomes a liability.

---

## Final Thoughts

These seven mistakes aren’t signs of bad engineering — they’re signs of real‑world pressure, tight deadlines, and imperfect information. The good news is that once you know where the traps are, you can design your process to avoid them entirely.

When done correctly, a risk assessment:
- improves safety  
- reduces downtime  
- supports better design decisions  
- builds trust with operators and maintenance teams  

In one recent case, a team had fully validated a safety function — but never evaluated tool change. Within the first week, operators were bypassing the system.  
Not because they were careless — but because the design didn’t match reality.

Good risk assessments don’t slow teams down — they make engineering faster, safer, and more predictable.
